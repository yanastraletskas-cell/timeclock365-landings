ПОСТ 1 — ПОНЕДЕЛЬНИК

Your time tracker might be the most expensive tool in your HR stack — and not in the way you expect.

Here's what's happening right now at most engineering companies: employees submit hours at the end of the day or the end of the week. They forget 20 minutes here, round up 15 minutes there. Your HR team spends hours before every payroll run chasing down discrepancies. And errors still slip through.

The problem isn't the people. It's the process.

Manual time entry is structurally inaccurate. When attendance depends on what an employee remembers and chooses to log, your payroll accuracy depends on that too. And payroll errors don't just cost money — they create compliance exposure, damage employee trust, and consume HR capacity every single cycle.

The fix isn't stricter policies or more reminders. It's removing the manual step entirely.

When door entry is automatically logged as an attendance record — from the moment an employee arrives — there's nothing to fill in. No timesheets to chase. No data to reconcile. Payroll runs on verified records, not estimates.

If your current time tracker still depends on manual input, it's not solving the problem. It's just giving the problem a better interface.

ПОСТ 2 — ВТОРНИК

A workforce management tip most operations managers learn the hard way:

Time tracking and facility access are not two separate problems. They're the same problem, handled by two separate systems that were never meant to talk to each other.

When an employee badges into the building, you already know everything you need:

— Who arrived

— At what time

— Which location or zone they entered

That's attendance data. The only reason it doesn't appear in your workforce system automatically is because your access control and your time tracker are disconnected.

Here's the practical question to ask before evaluating any new workforce tool: "Does this system capture attendance from door entry automatically?"

If the answer is no, you're still running two systems, paying two vendors, and manually bridging the gap between them — and that gap is where your errors and compliance risks live.

Unified workforce management isn't about buying more software. It's about eliminating the reconciliation work that happens between tools. One entry event. One record. No manual step in between.

    Competitor Profiles

    1. Clockify

    What they offer:

    Clockify is a free-to-start time tracking tool aimed at freelancers and small teams. It offers basic time logging, project tracking, and simple reporting. Its business model relies on upselling paid tiers for more advanced features, but the core product remains narrowly focused on time entry.

    Main weakness vs. TimeClock 365:

    Clockify has no door access control whatsoever. Attendance must be entered manually by employees — there is no automatic capture of who is physically present. Companies using Clockify still need a completely separate (and disconnected) access control system for their facilities.

    Marketing angle:

    > "Clockify tracks the hours your employees *say* they worked. TimeClock 365 tracks the hours they *actually* worked — automatically, from the moment they walk through the door."

    ---

    2. Hubstaff

    What they offer:

    Hubstaff is a remote workforce monitoring platform with GPS tracking, screenshots, and activity monitoring for distributed teams. It serves companies managing remote and field employees and positions itself as a productivity-oversight tool. Subscription pricing scales per user.

    Main weakness vs. TimeClock 365:

    Hubstaff uses GPS to track field workers but has no physical door access control. It cannot log or manage who enters a facility, integrate with access hardware (fingerprint/card/mobile readers), or turn a door entry event into an attendance record. Companies with office premises still operate two disconnected systems.

    Marketing angle:

    > "Hubstaff tells you where your employees are on a map. TimeClock 365 tells you who is actually in your building — and logs it as attendance automatically."

    ---

    3. Toggl

    What they offer:

    Toggl (Toggl Track) is a lightweight, user-friendly time tracking app built primarily for individuals and project-based teams. It emphasizes simplicity — one-click timers and clean reporting — and is popular among agencies and consultancies. It has no workforce management or access control capabilities.

    Main weakness vs. TimeClock 365:

    Toggl has no access control and no automatic attendance capture. It is entirely dependent on self-reported time entries, making it unsuitable for companies that need verified, hardware-backed attendance data. It is also not designed for managing mixed workforces (office + remote + field).

    Marketing angle:

    > "Toggl is built for freelancers who trust themselves. TimeClock 365 is built for operations managers who need verified attendance across an entire workforce — without asking employees to log anything."

    ---

    4. Time Doctor

    What they offer:

    Time Doctor is a remote employee monitoring platform focused on productivity tracking — it logs websites visited, apps used, and time on tasks. It is primarily aimed at remote teams and BPO companies. Its value proposition centers on proving that remote workers are productive during working hours.

    Main weakness vs. TimeClock 365:

    Time Doctor is a pure-software solution with no hardware integration and no door access control. It cannot monitor physical presence in an office, integrate with facility access systems, or generate attendance records tied to real-world entry events. It is essentially inapplicable to any company with a physical workplace requiring access management.

    Marketing angle:

    > "Time Doctor monitors screens. TimeClock 365 manages your actual workplace — who enters, when they arrived, and whether payroll is correct — all from one system."

    ---

    5. Zoho People

    What they offer:

    Zoho People is a broad HR management platform covering employee onboarding, leave management, performance reviews, and basic time tracking. It is part of the larger Zoho suite and appeals to companies already using Zoho products. Its strength is HR process management, not workforce presence tracking.

    Main weakness vs. TimeClock 365:

    Zoho People has no door access control integration. Its time tracking relies on manual check-ins or integrations with third-party tools, meaning attendance data is still subject to human error. Companies using Zoho People for HR still need a separate, disconnected system for physical access management.

    Marketing angle:

    > "Zoho People handles your HR processes. TimeClock 365 handles your HR *and* your physical access control — in one system, with attendance recorded automatically at the door."
